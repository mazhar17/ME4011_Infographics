R1
Irradiance Components & the Horizontal Closure (Parts 3–4)
Beam · Diffuse · Global · GHI = DNI·cos θz + DHI
▸

From Part 3: Global = Beam + Diffuse. From Part 4: on the horizontal plane the measured quantities close as GHI ≈ DNI·cos(θz) + DHI. Today's lecture repeats this decomposition — but on a tilted plane, where a third player joins: ground-reflected (albedo) irradiance.

Horizontal: GHI = DNI·cos θz + DHI   →   Tilted (today): Gt = Gt,b + Gt,d + Gt,r
  • Memory aid: tilting the plane "unlocks" the ground — a horizontal plane never sees ground reflection, a tilted one does. That is why a third term appears today.
  • Examination tip: subscripts tell the story: b = beam, d = diffuse, r = reflected, h = horizontal, t = tilted, n = normal (to the beam).
  • Common mistake: forgetting the cos θz projection when moving DNI onto the horizontal.
  • Conceptual relationship: every tilted-plane formula today starts from a horizontal quantity you already know how to measure (Part 4).
R2
Solar Geometry Essentials (Parts 1–2)
Zenith angle θz · altitude · equinox noon rule
▸
Solar zenith angle θz
Angle between the sun and the vertical (zenith). Solar altitude = 90° − θz.
Latitude φ & declination δ
Set where the sun stands. On the equinox (δ = 0), the noon solar elevation is exactly 90° − latitude — the geometric seed of today's "tilt = latitude" rule.
Tilt angle β
The angle of the panel from the horizontal — today's main design variable.
  • Memory aid: θz is measured from the vertical; altitude from the horizon; β from the ground. Three references, three angles.
  • Frequently confused: θ (incidence on the panel), θz (sun from vertical), β (panel from horizontal). Today's key link: θ = θz − β.
  • Quick reminder: equinox noon elevation = 90° − φ ⇒ tilting the panel by φ makes it face the sun square-on.
  • Practical shortcut: Dhaka φ ≈ 23.7° N — keep this number ready; it becomes the recommended tilt.
R3
Cosine Behaviour — the Mathematical Heart of This Lecture
cos 0° = 1 · cos 60° = 0.5 · cos 90° = 0
▸

Every formula today multiplies an irradiance by a cosine (or a cosine-based factor). Drag the cursor on the curve and internalize the shape: flat near 0° (small misalignment costs little), steep near 90° (oblique angles are punishing).

  • Memory anchors: cos 0° = 1, cos 25° ≈ 0.906, cos 45° ≈ 0.707, cos 60° = 0.5, cos 90° = 0.
  • Engineering intuition: a 10° pointing error near normal incidence loses only ~1.5% — but the same 10° near grazing loses everything. Alignment tolerance depends on where you operate on the curve.
  • Examination tip: when an answer looks wrong, first check whether you used degrees vs. radians in the cosine.
  • Common mistake: applying cos to the wrong angle — always ask "angle between the ray and the surface NORMAL".

The tilt ≈ latitude rule is a geometry-only optimum: it maximizes the annual beam capture for a fixed, equator-facing panel under a clear sky. Real optimizers (PVGIS, PVsyst) nudge it: monsoon climates with heavy diffuse shares favour slightly lower tilts (a flatter panel sees more sky dome), while soiling-prone sites favour slightly steeper tilts for rain self-cleaning. The rule is the anchor; the climate supplies the correction.

Compress the slide into one sentence: "β = φ for the year; steeper for winter; lower for summer." Attach Dhaka: φ ≈ 23.7° ⇒ recommended fixed tilt ≈ 24°.

Reading "steeper tilt for winter loads" backwards. In winter the sun rides LOW, so the panel must stand UP (steeper) to face it. Lower summer sun-height logic is the mirror image. If in doubt, sketch the noon sun and the panel normal.

Quote-ready: "For a fixed tilt angle, the maximum power over the course of a year is obtained when the tilt angle is equal to the latitude of the location" (PVEDUCATION.ORG, nd) — then add the two seasonal corollaries (steeper ↔ winter loads, lower ↔ summer).

Rooftop solar in Bangladesh is commonly installed at 20–25° facing south — the latitude rule in action. Off-grid telecom sites with critical winter loads deliberately exceed it (30–35°) to harden December–January output.

Seasonally adjusted mounting (changing β twice a year) recovers roughly half the gain of full tracking at a fraction of the cost — many small systems worldwide still use a summer/winter bolt-hole pair on the mounting frame.

Cosine Law in Solar Radiation

Lambert's Cosine Law explains how the effective irradiance on a surface varies with the angle of incidence (θ).

The mathematical expression is:

I = I0 cos θ

where:

  • I = Received irradiance on the surface
  • I0 = Irradiance perpendicular to the surface
  • θ = Angle between the incoming rays and the surface normal

As θ increases (i.e., rays become more oblique), the same energy is distributed over a larger area, reducing irradiance per unit area.

This principle is critical in solar energy calculations, particularly for tilted or tracking surfaces.

An IMPORTANT FIGURE

Stylized recreation of the lecture's world insolation figure (original attribution: CC BY-SA 3.0, commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=1770816). Hover the latitude bands for readouts.

What is the most important message this figure conveys for anyone designing solar energy systems around the world?
The figure's core message: the solar resource is latitude-structured. Insolation is highest in the subtropical belts (clear deserts near ±20–30°) and falls toward the poles as rays grow oblique (Lambert's cosine law acting on a planetary scale) and traverse more atmosphere. For a designer this means: (1) the available annual energy — and hence system size for a given demand — depends strongly on where you build; (2) the optimal fixed tilt tracks latitude, because tilting the panel "undoes" the local ray obliquity; (3) the same PV module yields very different energy in Dhaka (≈24° N, ~4–5 kWh/m²/day) than in northern Europe — solar design is always site-specific, never copy-paste.

Tilt Angle = Latitude: Explained with Geometry

  • Solar rays strike the Earth at different angles depending on latitude.
  • Higher latitudes (region "a"): longer atmospheric path; larger surface area, hence less irradiance per unit area.
  • Near the equator (region "b"): rays are more perpendicular to the surface; higher energy density.
  • For fixed solar panels: maximum annual energy gain occurs when tilt angle equals local latitude.
  • On equinox: solar elevation at noon = 90° − latitude.

Application of Cosine Law in Solar Energy Systems

Optimal Panel Orientation
To maximize received irradiance (I), solar panels are oriented to minimize θ — i.e., face the Sun directly.
Tilt Angle Design
The Cosine Law guides the selection of fixed or seasonally adjustable tilt angles for maximizing annual solar gain.
Tracking Systems
Solar trackers continuously adjust panel orientation to maintain θ ≈ 0°, thereby ensuring I ≈ I₀ throughout the day.
Shading and Performance Losses
Even partial obliquity reduces irradiance due to cos θ behavior, emphasizing the need for shading analysis and optimal array spacing.
Simulation Tools
Solar simulation software (e.g., PVsyst, SAM) uses Cosine Law in modeling incident radiation and energy yield.

Direct Component of Solar Irradiance (Heinemann, 2019, Slide 5)

According to (Heinemann, 2019, Slide 5), direct component over a horizontal plane,

Gb,h = Gb,n × cos θz   (1)

Direct component over a tilted plane,

Gb,t = Gb,n × cos θ   (2)

Substituting (1) in (2), we get

Gb,t = Gb,h × cos θ⁄cos θz   (3)

Isotropic means the sky dome is assumed equally bright in every direction, so the diffuse reaching the panel depends only on how much dome it sees — the factor (1 + cos β)/2. Real skies are anisotropic: brighter around the sun (circumsolar diffuse) and along the horizon. That is why the lecture flags the approach as "usually too simple"; advanced models (e.g., Perez) add those terms, but the isotropic formula remains the standard first estimate and exam workhorse.

The three factors of this lecture are the DNA of every transposition model in professional software: cos θ/cos θz (beam geometry), (1 + cos β)/2 (sky view), ρ(1 − cos β)/2 (ground view). PVGIS and PVsyst refine the diffuse term with anisotropic models, but structure and limits are identical — master these three and you can sanity-check any simulator's output by hand.

Learn the two view factors as a pair that always sums to 1: sky share (1 + cos β)/2 + ground share (1 − cos β)/2 = 1. Flat panel: all sky, no ground. Vertical panel: half sky, half ground. Everything between interpolates smoothly.

Three classics: (1) mixing up θ, θz and β — θ is measured from the PANEL normal, θz from the VERTICAL, β from the HORIZONTAL; (2) using θ = θz − β blindly — it holds for an equator-facing panel with the sun in the same vertical plane (e.g., solar noon), not for arbitrary sun azimuths; (3) forgetting that at β = 0 the albedo term vanishes exactly — a horizontal panel cannot see the ground.

Write the four formulas as a block before solving anything: (1) Gb,h = Gb,n cos θz; (2) Gb,t = Gb,n cos θ; (3) Gb,t = Gb,h·cos θ/cos θz with θ = θz − β; diffuse Gd,t = Gd,h(1+cos β)/2; albedo Gr,t = Ghρ(1−cos β)/2; total Gt = Gt,b + Gt,d + Gt,r. Most marks are for the correct factor applied to the correct component.

A Dhaka rooftop study: take GHI/DHI from a dataset (POWER/PVGIS), split out Gb,h = GHI − DHI, transpose all three components to β ≈ 24°, and you have the plane-of-array irradiance that a PV yield estimate multiplies by module efficiency. This hand calculation is exactly what the Dataset Explorer and Problem Solving tabs rehearse.

Vertical bifacial fences in snowy countries exploit the albedo term twice: fresh snow (ρ up to ~0.9) plus β = 90° maximizes ρ(1−cos β)/2 = ρ/2 on each face — winter output can rival a conventional tilted array's.

    ⭐ Key Takeaways

    • A tilted plane receives three components: direct (beam), diffuse (sky), and reflected (albedo) — Gt = Gt,b + Gt,d + Gt,r (Heinemann, 2019).
    • Lambert's Cosine Law, I = I₀ cos θ: maximum energy is absorbed when sunlight strikes the panel perpendicularly (θ ≈ 0°) — "Same amount of incoming radiant energy covers different surface areas."
    • Beam transposes by geometry: Gb,t = Gb,h·cos θ/cos θz with θ = θz − β.
    • Diffuse follows the sky view: Gd,t = Gd,h(1 + cos β)/2 — under the isotropic-sky assumption the lecture flags as "usually too simple".
    • Ground reflection follows the ground view and the albedo: Gr,t = Gh·ρ·(1 − cos β)/2 — fresh snow reflects far more than grass.
    • Optimal annual tilt ≈ local latitude; steeper tilts favour winter, lower tilts favour summer (PVEDUCATION.ORG, nd).
    • Tilted-plane datasets come from NASA's POWER Data Access Viewer and PVGIS.

    🔗 Most Important Relationships

    🛠 Engineering Applications

    • Fixed-tilt PV design (β ≈ φ, Dhaka ≈ 24°).
    • Winter-critical systems → steeper β; summer-heavy loads → lower β.
    • Trackers hold θ ≈ 0° to keep I ≈ I₀.
    • Snowy/bright surroundings → albedo term earns real energy (bifacial designs).
    • POWER & PVGIS supply the datasets; the formulas here sanity-check them.
