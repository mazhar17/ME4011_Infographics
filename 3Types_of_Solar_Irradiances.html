Understanding Solar Irradiance

GHI, DNI & DHI

An Interactive Learning Module for Undergraduate Mechanical & Renewable Energy Engineers

Solar irradiance is the power of solar radiation received per unit area, measured in W/m².

GHI = DHI + DNI × cos(θz)

The Three Types of Solar Irradiance

Understanding the components of solar radiation reaching the Earth's surface.

Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI)

The total amount of shortwave radiation received from above by a surface horizontal to the ground.

GHI = Direct + Diffuse

Applications:

Rooftop Solar PV Utility-scale PV Building Energy Analysis

Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI)

The amount of solar radiation received per unit area by a surface that is always held perpendicular (normal) to the straight rays from the sun.

Applications:

Parabolic Trough (CSP) Solar Tower 2-Axis Tracking PV

Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance (DHI)

The radiation received from the sky dome (excluding the solar disk) that has been scattered by the atmosphere and clouds.

Applications:

Daylighting Bifacial PV Cloudy Weather Modeling

Parameter Comparison

Parameter Measurement Surface Direction Dominant Weather State Key Technology
GHI (Global Horizontal) Horizontal Hemispherical (All) Overall clear & bright Standard PV Panels
DNI (Direct Normal) Tracking (Perpendicular) Straight from Sun disk Extremely clear skies Concentrated Solar Power (CSP)
DHI (Diffuse Horizontal) Horizontal (shaded) Scattered from sky dome Overcast / Cloudy Daylighting / Architecture

Solar Energy Applications

Matching the right technology to the right irradiance parameter.

🏠

Rooftop & Utility PV

Relies on GHI (or Plane of Array irradiance derived from GHI) because flat panels capture both direct beams and scattered light.

🗼

Concentrated Solar Power (CSP)

Relies on DNI. Lenses and mirrors can only focus parallel rays. Scattered (diffuse) light cannot be concentrated.

🏢

Daylighting & Architecture

Relies heavily on DHI. Building designers prefer diffuse light to illuminate spaces without the harsh glare of direct beams.

Resource Assessment

Engineers must use long-term datasets (like TMY - Typical Meteorological Year) containing all three parameters to accurately model power plant output.

Engineering Decision Tree

Which parameter should you prioritize for your project?

What is your project goal? ▼
Use GHI. PV panels capture all incident light. For tilted panels, you will calculate Plane of Array (POA) irradiance primarily derived from GHI.
Use DNI. Parabolic troughs and solar towers can only concentrate parallel light rays coming directly from the sun.
Use DHI. Diffuse light is best for indoor daylighting to prevent severe glare and localized overheating.

Real-World Examples

🇧🇩

Bangladesh

High GHI, moderate DNI. Excellent conditions for widespread Rooftop and Utility Solar PV deployment.

🇲🇦

Morocco

Extremely high DNI with clear desert skies. World-class location for massive Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) plants.

🇬🇧

Northern Europe

High fraction of DHI due to clouds. Standard PV and passive building daylighting are essential engineering focus points.
