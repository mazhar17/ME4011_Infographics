R1
Solar Geometry Essentials (from Part 1)
Declination angle · hour angle · day length
▸

Part 1 answered WHERE the sun is. Three quantities locate it:

Declination angle δ
The angle between the Earth–Sun line and the equatorial plane. It varies between −23.45° and +23.45° over the year (≈ 0° at the equinoxes, +23.45° at the June solstice).
Hour angle ω
The angular displacement of the sun from the local meridian: 15° per hour, negative before solar noon, 0° at solar noon, positive after.
Day length
Set by the sunrise/sunset hour angle ωs = cos⁻¹(−tan φ · tan δ); day length = 2ωs/15 hours. At the equinoxes it is 12 h everywhere.
  • Memory aid: "δ for Drift of the seasons, ω for the clock" — δ changes day-to-day, ω changes minute-to-minute.
  • Examination tip: ω = 15° × (solar time − 12). A time of 10:00 gives ω = −30°. Sign errors are the #1 lost mark.
  • Frequently confused: declination δ is NOT latitude φ. δ belongs to the sun–earth geometry; φ belongs to your site (Dhaka ≈ 23.7° N).
  • Engineering intuition: geometry tells you where the sun is; this lecture (irradiance) tells you how much energy arrives from that direction.
  • Quick reminder: at the equinoxes δ ≈ 0° and day length = 12 h at every latitude.
R2
The Electromagnetic Spectrum and the Visible Range
Wavelength bands · why "300–780 nm" matters
▸

Solar radiation is electromagnetic radiation. The bands that matter for solar energy: ultraviolet (< ~380 nm), visible (~380–780 nm, violet→red), and infrared (> ~780 nm). PV spectral-response figures in this lecture are plotted against wavelength in nanometres (nm).

  • Memory aid: "UV – Vis – IR, short to long" — wavelength grows left to right in every chart of this lecture.
  • Quick reminder: shorter wavelength = more energetic photons. Blue (~450 nm) photons are more energetic than red (~700 nm).
  • Conceptual relationship: Rayleigh scattering strength rises steeply as wavelength falls — this drives the "Blue Shift" in the diffuse spectrum you will meet in Diffuse Radiation.
  • Common mistake: reading "300–780 nm" as frequency. All lecture charts use wavelength (nm) on the x-axis.
R3
Blackbody Radiation and the Solar Spectrum
Planck curve · T ≈ 5,778 K · Wien's peak
▸

The sun radiates approximately as a blackbody at T ≈ 5,778 K. A blackbody at that temperature emits a smooth, continuous spectrum peaking near ~500 nm (Wien's displacement law, λmax ≈ 2898/T μm·K) — right in the visible band. This blackbody curve is the black reference curve in the lecture's "Blackbody vs. Diffuse Spectral Irradiance" figure: the theoretical maximum reaching the Top of Atmosphere.

  • Memory aid: "5-7-7-8 — the sun's licence plate." T ≈ 5,778 K appears again in the Diffuse Spectral Irradiance Analysis.
  • Engineering intuition: the blackbody curve is the ceiling; every real curve (ground-level, diffuse) sits below it after atmospheric losses.
  • Examination tip: smooth + continuous = blackbody/top-of-atmosphere; bumpy or shifted = ground-level after scattering/absorption.
  • Frequently confused: the blackbody curve is emitted power per wavelength, not total power. Total power is the area under the curve.
R4
Energy vs. Power and Their Units
J · W · W/m² · kWh/m²
▸
Energy — joule (J)
Capacity to do work. 1 kWh = 3.6 MJ.
Power — watt (W)
Rate of energy flow: 1 W = 1 J/s. Power is instantaneous.
Power density — W/m²
Power crossing a unit area. This is the unit of solar irradiance in this lecture.
Energy density — kWh/m²
Power density integrated over time. This is the unit of accumulated solar radiation (e.g., kWh/m² per day).
Power (W) = Energy (J) / time (s)  ⇔  Energy = ∫ Power · dt
  • Memory aid: "W is a speed, kWh is a distance." Irradiance (W/m²) is the speedometer; radiation (kWh/m²) is the odometer.
  • Practical shortcut: a constant 1,000 W/m² for 1 hour delivers exactly 1 kWh/m².
  • Common mistake: writing "kW/h" — the correct energy unit is kWh (kilowatt × hour), not kilowatt per hour.
  • Examination tip: whenever a question asks for kWh/m², look for a summation or integration of W/m² values over time — that is the core relationship of this lecture.

Solar Radiation

"often called solar resource"(EERE, 2013)
"a general term for the electromagnetic radiation emitted by the sun"(EERE, 2013)

Different types exist — this lecture classifies them. Assessment of solar radiation is critical for solar energy projects.

"Solar Irradiance" or "insolation" (Stine and Geyer, 2001, Chapter 2)

"The rate at which solar energy reaches a unit area at the earth"(Stine and Geyer, 2001)

According to (Stine and Geyer, 2001):

"The units of measure for irradiance are watts per square meter (W/m²)"(Stine and Geyer, 2001)

"solar radiation is simply the integration or summation of solar irradiance over a time period."(Stine and Geyer, 2001)

Irradiance is the design driver of every solar system: PV module power ratings are defined at a Standard Test Condition irradiance of 1,000 W/m². Because irradiance "can vary over time" (Stine and Geyer, 2001), engineers never size a plant on a single instantaneous value — they integrate it into daily/annual solar radiation (kWh/m²) to predict energy yield and revenue.

Anchor the two words to their units: irradiance ↔ W/m² (rate, instantaneous), radiation ↔ kWh/m² or MJ/m² (energy, integrated). If the unit contains "hour" or "joule", it is the integrated quantity. Say it as: "radiation = irradiance × time (summed)".

Students often write "the irradiance today was 5 kWh/m²." Wrong quantity! 5 kWh/m² is a day's integrated solar radiation. Irradiance is the instantaneous rate — "the rate at which solar energy reaches a unit area at the earth" (Stine and Geyer, 2001) — and must carry W/m². Check the unit before you name the quantity.

Expect a definition question quoting Stine and Geyer (2001). Reproduce the phrase "the rate at which solar energy reaches a unit area at the earth" and state the unit W/m². For numerical questions, "find the solar radiation" almost always means: sum (or integrate) the given irradiance values over the stated time period.

A pyranometer on a Dhaka rooftop logs irradiance every minute. The monitoring software integrates those W/m² samples into daily kWh/m² — the figure used to compute the plant's Performance Ratio and to verify the EPC contractor's yield guarantee. The same integration you performed with the drag-window above happens automatically in every commercial solar plant.

The word insolation — used by Stine and Geyer (2001) as a synonym for solar irradiance — comes from Latin insolare, "to expose to the sun." It is unrelated to "insulation," despite sounding nearly identical; mixing the two up in a report is a classic (and memorable) engineering typo.

Direct or Beam Radiation

"The solar radiation that reaches the Earth's surface without being diffused is called direct beam solar radiation."(EERE, 2013)

A concentrating collector (parabolic trough, dish, heliostat/power tower) uses mirrors or lenses to redirect rays onto a small receiver. Optical concentration only works when incoming rays share one well-defined direction — precisely the property of beam radiation. Diffuse light arrives from every direction of the sky dome, so a concentrator's narrow acceptance angle rejects almost all of it. Consequence: Concentrating Solar Power (CSP) is sited where beam resource (DNI) is high — deserts — and evaluated with DNI data, never with global values alone.

Link the definition to the shadow test: if you can see a crisp shadow outline, beam radiation dominates. "Without being diffused" (EERE, 2013) is the key phrase — memorize it as straight-line sunlight.

Assuming beam radiation is always the larger component. On a thick, cloudy day beam can be reduced by 100% (EERE, 2013) — i.e., zero — while diffuse light still illuminates the ground. In monsoon Dhaka, diffuse frequently dominates the global total.

Quote the EERE (2013) definition verbatim, including "without being diffused." If asked which component a parabolic trough uses, answer: beam/direct radiation (measured as DNI), because concentration requires a single ray direction.

Beam (direct-normal) irradiance is measured with a pyrheliometer mounted on a two-axis sun tracker that keeps the instrument pointed at the solar disc. Feasibility studies for CSP plants require at least a year of such DNI measurements — a direct industrial consequence of this definition.

Diffuse Spectral Irradiance Analysis

Comparison of Spectral Profiles (lecture figure: Blackbody vs. Diffuse Spectral Irradiance) — move your pointer across the chart for a wavelength readout.

Blackbody Curve (Black)
Represents the theoretical maximum energy emitted by the sun (T ≈ 5,778 K) reaching the Top of Atmosphere. It is smooth and continuous.
Diffuse Curve (Blue)
Represents scattered sunlight reaching the ground (characteristic of "skylight" or cloudy conditions).
The "Blue Shift"
Higher intensity in short wavelengths (300–500 nm) due to Rayleigh scattering, which scatters blue light more effectively than red.

Air molecules are far smaller than the wavelength of visible light, so they scatter light in the Rayleigh regime, where scattering strength grows steeply as wavelength falls (∝ 1/λ⁴). A 450 nm blue photon is scattered roughly (700/450)⁴ ≈ 5.7× more strongly than a 700 nm red photon. Blue light is therefore redirected all over the sky dome and reaches your eye from every direction — the sky looks blue, and the diffuse spectrum shows the "Blue Shift" peak at 300–500 nm seen in the lecture figure. At sunset the beam path through air is long, the blue is scattered out of the beam, and the remaining direct light looks red.

"Since diffuse solar irradiance cannot be concentrated, only flat-plate (non-concentrating) solar collectors and some low-temperature types of concentrators (having wide acceptance angles) can collect diffuse solar irradiance"(Stine and Geyer, 2001)
"Few of the collectors used in industrial applications can utilize the diffuse component of solar radiation."(Stine and Geyer, 2001)

Spectral Sensitivity by Technology

Technology Comparison (lecture figure: Spectral Response vs. Solar Spectrum) — toggle the curves and hover for readouts.

Amorphous Si (a-Si)
Blue/Green Range. Active only in the visible spectrum (∼300–780 nm). Good for indoor/diffuse light but misses IR energy.
Crystalline Si (c-Si)
Standard Broad Range. Captures visible + Near-Infrared (up to ∼1100 nm). The industry standard for outdoor panels.
CIGS / Thin Film
Widest Response. Can be tuned to capture deep infrared (∼1200+ nm), potentially harvesting more photons.

Design implication for Bangladesh: during the June–October monsoon, Dhaka's sky is diffuse-dominant and the resource spectrum collapses into the visible band (300–750 nm). Technology selection and yield simulation for Bangladeshi solar projects must therefore weigh diffuse-light spectral performance — not just the c-Si standard-test-condition rating obtained under a full direct-sun spectrum.

The diffuse component sets a hard technology boundary: since it "cannot be concentrated" (Stine and Geyer, 2001), every concentrating technology loses the diffuse share of the resource entirely. In a climate where 50–70% of annual global radiation arrives diffuse (monsoon South Asia, northern Europe), flat-plate PV/thermal collectors capture a resource that CSP physically cannot touch — often the deciding factor in technology selection.

Memorize the seven scattering agents with the mnemonic "A W C D P F V" — "All Wet Clouds Drop Pollutants From Volcanoes": Air molecules, Water vapor, Clouds, Dust, Pollutants, Forest fires, Volcanoes. Note the list mixes permanent agents (air molecules) with episodic ones (forest fires, volcanoes).

Believing diffuse radiation is "useless." It is unconcentratable, not unusable: flat-plate collectors and PV modules collect it well — it "is what permits us to see in the shade" (Stine and Geyer, 2001). A second trap: assuming c-Si is always the best PV choice; under a monsoon (diffuse) spectrum its IR advantage "is nullified... because that IR energy simply isn't there."

Three favourite exam items from this section: (1) list all seven scattering agents from EERE (2013); (2) explain the "Blue Shift" (300–500 nm, Rayleigh scattering, blue scattered more effectively than red); (3) justify which PV technology suits a diffuse/monsoon climate — answer a-Si "Ideal Match", c-Si "Under-utilized", quoting the spectral ranges (a-Si ∼300–780 nm, c-Si up to ∼1100 nm, CIGS ∼1200+ nm).

Diffuse horizontal irradiance (DHI) is measured with a pyranometer fitted with a shadow band or tracking ball that blocks the direct beam. Simulation tools (PVsyst, SAM) then use DHI with a transposition model to estimate how much diffuse light a tilted Dhaka rooftop array actually receives — a required step in every Bangladeshi rooftop-solar feasibility study.

After the 1991 Mount Pinatubo eruption, volcanic aerosols measurably increased the diffuse fraction of sunlight worldwide for about two years — a real, planet-scale demonstration of "Volcanoes" acting as a scattering agent in the EERE (2013) list. Interestingly, the extra diffuse light boosted photosynthesis in shaded leaves and briefly increased the global land carbon sink.

Global horizontal irradiance (GHI) is the quantity a plain, horizontal pyranometer measures — one instrument, both components. That is why GHI is the backbone of solar resource maps and TMY files, while separating it into beam and diffuse (needed for tilted or concentrating collectors) requires either extra instruments or decomposition models.

Remember the two anchor numbers of this slide as the "10/100 rule": beam reduced by 10% on clear, dry days, by 100% during thick, cloudy days (EERE, 2013). Everything real sits between those ends — and diffuse fills part of the gap, so global never falls as far as beam does.

Writing Global = Beam − Diffuse, or treating "100% reduction of beam" as "zero global radiation." When beam is fully extinguished, diffuse remains — a thick, cloudy sky still delivers usable global radiation (often 100–300 W/m² at midday).

Two near-certain items: state the definition "the sum of the diffuse and direct solar radiation" (EERE, 2013), and reproduce the 10%/100% beam-reduction figures with their sky conditions. For numericals, always compute global as the simple sum of the two given components.

Grid-scale PV yield estimates for Bangladesh start from GHI maps (Global Solar Atlas). A developer checks GHI first, then asks for the beam/diffuse split only when considering trackers or concentrators — mirroring exactly this lecture's Global = Beam + Diffuse decomposition.

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • Solar radiation is "often called solar resource" (EERE, 2013) and its assessment is critical for solar energy projects.
  • Solar irradiance ("insolation") is "the rate at which solar energy reaches a unit area at the earth" — units W/m² (Stine and Geyer, 2001).
  • Three types: Beam or Direct Radiation · Diffuse or Indirect Radiation · Global Radiation.
  • Diffuse radiation comes from scattering by air molecules, water vapor, clouds, dust, pollutants, forest fires and volcanoes (EERE, 2013) — and it cannot be concentrated.
  • Atmospheric conditions can reduce direct beam radiation by 10% (clear, dry) to 100% (thick, cloudy) (EERE, 2013).
  • TMY datasets feed computer simulations of solar energy conversion and building systems — but must not be used for worst-case/extreme-event design.

🛠 Engineering Applications

  • Concentrating collectors (CSP) → need beam (DNI); infeasible where diffuse dominates.
