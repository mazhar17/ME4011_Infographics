R1
The Three Types of Solar Irradiances (from Part 3)
Beam · Diffuse · Global = Beam + Diffuse
▸
Beam or Direct Radiation
Reaches the surface without being diffused — one well-defined direction; casts sharp shadows; the only component that can be concentrated.
Diffuse or Indirect Radiation
Scattered by the atmosphere (air molecules, water vapor, clouds, dust, pollutants, forest fires, volcanoes); arrives from the whole sky dome.
Global Radiation
The sum of the diffuse and direct solar radiation.
Global = Beam + Diffuse

Today's lecture assigns one instrument to each: pyranometer → global, shaded pyranometer → diffuse, pyrheliometer → beam.

  • Memory aid: "Beam is a line, diffuse is a dome, global is both." Each instrument's geometry copies its component: narrow tube = line; open dome = dome + line; shaded dome = dome only.
  • Examination tip: in dataset language the trio is DNI (beam), DHI (diffuse), GHI (global) — today you learn which instrument produces each column.
  • Common mistake: forgetting that beam can drop to zero under thick cloud while diffuse (and hence global) remains.
  • Conceptual relationship: at a measuring station, GHI ≈ DNI·cos(θz) + DHI, where θz is the solar zenith angle — the consistency check used to validate real stations.
R2
Irradiance (Rate) vs. Solar Radiation (Integrated Energy)
W/m² · kWh/m² — reading instrument outputs correctly
▸

Solar irradiance is the rate at which solar energy reaches a unit area — measured in W/m², an instantaneous quantity that varies over time. Integrating (summing) it over a period gives solar radiation in kWh/m² (or MJ/m²). Every instrument in today's lecture outputs the rate (W/m²); dataloggers integrate it into energy. Sunshine-hour recorders output something different again: a duration (hours).

Radiation (kWh/m²) = ∫ Irradiance (W/m²) dt  ·  Sunshine hours = time with bright sunshine
  • Memory aid: "W is a speed, kWh is a distance, hours is a stopwatch." Three different output types — irradiance, energy, duration.
  • Practical shortcut: 1,000 W/m² held for 1 h = exactly 1 kWh/m².
  • Common mistake: writing that a Campbell-Stokes recorder measures W/m² — it records hours of bright sunshine, not irradiance.
  • Examination tip: check the unit before naming the quantity: W/m² = irradiance; kWh/m² = radiation; h = sunshine duration.
R3
Sun Position and Tracking Basics (from Parts 1–2)
Why some instruments must follow the sun
▸

The sun's direction changes continuously — about 15° per hour in hour angle, plus seasonal changes in declination (±23.45°). An instrument that must stay pointed at the solar disk (like a pyrheliometer with its narrow field of view) therefore needs a solar tracker; an instrument that accepts the whole sky (pyranometer, 180° field of view) does not. A shading ball that must continuously hide the sun from a diffuse sensor also rides on a tracker.

  • Engineering intuition: narrow field of view ⇒ must track; hemispherical field of view ⇒ can sit still. Geometry dictates mechanics.
  • Memory aid: "Tube tracks, dome dozes."
  • Frequently confused: a tracker aligns an instrument; a shading ball on a tracker deliberately blocks the sun — same mechanism, opposite purpose.
  • Quick reminder: dual-axis tracking handles both the daily arc (hour angle) and the seasonal height (declination).
R4
The Thermopile — How Heat Becomes a Voltage
Seebeck effect · the sensing heart of both instruments
▸

A thermocouple is a junction of two dissimilar metals that produces a small voltage proportional to a temperature difference (the Seebeck effect). A thermopile stacks many thermocouples in series to multiply that voltage. In a pyranometer or pyrheliometer, sunlight heats a black absorber; the thermopile between the hot absorber and the cool housing generates microvolts–millivolts, which a calibration constant converts to W/m².

Irradiance G (W/m²) = Voutput / Sensitivity  (e.g., µV per W/m²)
  • Memory aid: "Pile of thermocouples = thermoPILE = more volts."
  • Engineering intuition: the black surface answers "how much light?", the thermopile answers "how hot did it get?", the calibration answers "so how many W/m²?"
  • Examination tip: a typical exam conversion: output 7.65 mV at sensitivity 8.5 µV/(W/m²) → 7,650/8.5 = 900 W/m². Watch the µV↔mV step!
  • Common mistake: thinking the thermopile measures temperature itself — it measures a temperature difference.

Measurement of the Global Solar Irradiance — Pyranometer - 1

A pyranometer is a scientific instrument used to measure global solar irradiance on a flat (horizontal or tilted) surface.

According to (Duffie and Beckman, 2013), "a pyranometer is a type of actinometer used to measure broadband solar irradiance on a planar surface."(Duffie and Beckman, 2013)

It is designed to measure the solar radiation flux density, typically in units of W/m², over a hemispherical field of view (180°).

The measurement includes both:

  • Direct solar irradiance (sunlight received in a straight line from the sun), and
  • Diffuse solar irradiance (sunlight scattered by the atmosphere and clouds).

Pyranometers are widely used in meteorology, climatology, and solar energy research to quantify the availability of solar energy for PV system design and performance evaluation.

The pyranometer's product — GHI — is the single most-used number in PV engineering: performance ratio, capacity-factor estimates and yield guarantees all divide plant output by pyranometer-measured irradiation. That is why plant contracts specify the instrument class (ISO 9060), calibration interval, and even cleaning schedule: a dusty dome under-reads irradiance and silently inflates the apparent performance ratio.

Etymology as memory aid: pyr (fire) + ano (upward/sky) + meter — "measures fire from the whole sky." The 180° hemispherical field of view is the phrase to attach: whole sky ⇒ global ⇒ direct + diffuse together.

Believing a pyranometer measures only direct sunshine. Wrong — its hemispherical (180°) view means the measurement includes both direct and diffuse irradiance. On an overcast day it still reads the diffuse component. (Also don't confuse it with the pyrheliometer — one letter group, completely different geometry.)

Quote-ready line: "a pyranometer is a type of actinometer used to measure broadband solar irradiance on a planar surface" (Duffie and Beckman, 2013). Add: flux density in W/m², hemispherical field of view (180°), includes both direct and diffuse — those four phrases are the full-marks answer.

Every grid-scale PV plant in Bangladesh installs at least two reference pyranometers (horizontal for GHI, in-plane for POA irradiance). Their integrated readings feed the daily performance-ratio report that operators send to lenders — the pyranometer is literally the cash-register of the plant.

The double glass dome is not decoration: the outer dome shields wind and rain, while the inner dome blocks thermal (longwave) radiation exchange with the cold sky that would otherwise chill the sensor and corrupt the reading — a subtle source of error called the "thermal offset."

Direct or Beam Radiation — Pyrheliometer - 1

A pyrheliometer measures only the direct beam solar irradiance by restricting incoming light to a narrow collimated field of view.

Sunlight enters through a precision aperture and strikes a thermopile, generating a measurable voltage.

The instrument must be mounted on a solar tracker to maintain alignment with the sun throughout the day.

Footnote: A collimated field of view is a small angular window—typically around 5.7°—aligned with the solar disk to exclude diffuse sky radiation. See (Duffie and Beckman, 2013).(Duffie and Beckman, 2013)

Concentrating Solar Power can use only the component a mirror can aim — the beam. A pyrheliometer is the sole ground instrument that measures that component directly, so CSP prefeasibility requires a year or more of tracked pyrheliometer (DNI) data. Financial models are so sensitive to DNI that a ±2% measurement bias can swing a project's equity return by more than a percentage point — instrument class and calibration traceability become bankability issues, not lab details.

Helio = sun (the disc itself). Pyr-helio-meter stares at the sun through a tube; pyr-ano-meter watches the whole sky. Attach the numbers: tube = 5.7°, dome = 180°.

Swapping the two instruments in an exam answer. Checklist to avoid it: tracker mentioned? → pyrheliometer. "Hemispherical/180°"? → pyranometer. Also wrong: assuming the pyrheliometer reads global minus diffuse — it physically excludes diffuse sky with its collimated field of view rather than subtracting it.

Three-line full-marks answer: (1) measures only direct beam irradiance via a narrow collimated field of view (~5.7°, aligned with the solar disk to exclude diffuse sky radiation); (2) sunlight enters a precision aperture and strikes a thermopile generating a measurable voltage; (3) mounted on a solar tracker to maintain alignment throughout the day.

Solar resource stations (e.g., BSRN-class or SolarMet stations installed for World Bank resource mapping in Bangladesh) mount the pyrheliometer on the same dual-axis tracker that carries the shading ball for the diffuse pyranometer — one tracker, two jobs: point one instrument at the sun, hide the sun from the other.

Campbell–Stokes Recorder

A classical meteorological device used to record the duration of bright sunshine over a 24-hour period.

It employs a solid glass sphere — sometimes called a Stokes sphere — which acts as a lens to focus direct solar radiation onto a specially designed calibrated card.

When the sun is sufficiently intense, the focused rays burn a trace on the card. The length of this burn correlates with the duration of bright sunshine.

Originally invented by John Francis Campbell in 1853, it was later improved in 1879 by Sir George Gabriel Stokes (Middleton, 1966).

Although increasingly replaced by automated sensors, it remains in use for its simplicity, no power requirement, and long historical data continuity (World Meteorological Organization, 2008).

🔍 Why Does the Sphere Burn the Card?

The glass sphere is a lens: it concentrates the incoming rays onto a tiny focal spot. Concentration only works on radiation with a single, well-defined direction — the direct beam. Recall Part 3: diffuse solar irradiance cannot be concentrated. So under overcast (diffuse-only) skies the focal spot never gets hot enough to scorch, and the card stays clean — the recorder is, in effect, a beam detector with a paper datalogger.

🗂 Seasonal Cards — Why Three Shapes?

The burn trace follows the sun's daily arc across the sphere's focal surface, and that arc shifts with the season (Part 2's sun paths!). Recorders therefore use three sets of curved cards in different holder slots: long curved summer cards (high arc), straight equinox cards, and short curved winter cards (low arc). Choosing the wrong slot puts the focus off the card — a classic observer error.

The recorder's genius is that it needs no calibration constant, no power and no electronics — the sun writes its own record. Its weakness is the flip side: the "sufficiently intense to burn" threshold is set by card physics (humidity, card batch) and the reading by a human eye, so its data carry a subjective, station-dependent bias that modern electronic sensors were designed to eliminate.

Dates mnemonic: "Campbell 1853 creates, Stokes 1879 strengthens" — inventor first (John Francis Campbell), improver second (Sir George Gabriel Stokes), citation (Middleton, 1966). And the three survival reasons rhyme as "simple, powerless, continuous": simplicity, no power requirement, long historical data continuity (WMO, 2008).

Writing that the Campbell-Stokes recorder measures irradiance in W/m². It does not — it records duration (hours of bright sunshine) over a 24-hour period. The output is a length of burn on a card, converted to hours; there is no flux-density reading anywhere in the instrument.

Likely question: "Explain the working principle of a Campbell-Stokes Recorder." Structure: glass (Stokes) sphere acts as lens → focuses direct solar radiation onto a calibrated card → sufficiently intense sun burns a trace → burn length correlates with bright-sunshine duration → 24-hour record; add the inventor/improver dates and the three reasons it remains in use.

Bangladesh Meteorological Department stations have logged sunshine hours for decades with Campbell-Stokes recorders. Those long records let engineers estimate solar radiation at sites with no pyranometer history (via Angström-type sunshine-to-radiation correlations) — often the only long-term solar data available for feasibility studies.

Campbell's original 1853 device charred a bowl of wood; Stokes' 1879 improvement introduced the card-in-metal-frame design still manufactured today — one of the longest production runs of any scientific instrument, ~145+ years and counting.

Modern Measurement of Diffuse Solar Radiation — Shaded Pyranometers and Diffuse Sensors

Diffuse radiation is measured using a pyranometer fitted with a shadow band, shading ring, or shading ball to block the direct beam from the sun.

This ensures that only the diffuse sky component is measured across the hemispherical field of view.

Modern shading systems are often automated and mounted on sun trackers, which dynamically adjust to block the sun throughout the day.

Advanced diffuse irradiance sensors offer high temporal resolution and are calibrated according to ISO 9060:2018 Class A (high-precision) standards.

Diffuse measurements are essential for:

  • Designing solar PV systems under cloudy conditions.
  • Validating radiative transfer models.
  • Computing the clearness index and solar resource quality.

Modern Measurement of Beam (Direct) Solar Radiation — Pyrheliometers with Sun Tracking Systems

Beam or direct normal irradiance (DNI) is measured using a high-precision pyrheliometer with a narrow field of view (5.7°).

The sensor is mounted on a dual-axis solar tracker that ensures continuous alignment with the solar disk.

Modern pyrheliometers use thermopile detectors with calibrated response across the solar spectrum and produce data in W/m² at sub-minute intervals.

Instruments are compliant with ISO 9060:2018 Class A and evaluated under WMO performance criteria for traceable DNI data.

Accurate DNI measurement is critical for:

  • Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) and solar thermal system design.
  • Performance monitoring and bankability studies.
  • Sky condition classification and atmospheric modeling.

Modern Advancements in Sunshine Duration Measurement — Electronic Sensors and Automated Systems

Modern sunshine measurement instruments use electronic sensors, such as:

  • Silicon photodiodes – respond rapidly to changes in light intensity.
  • Thermopile sensors – measure irradiance over a wider spectral range.

These instruments detect sunshine based on a predefined irradiance threshold (e.g., 120 W/m² as per WMO guidelines) and operate with high temporal resolution (e.g., 1-minute intervals).

Systems are often integrated with automatic weather stations (AWS), enabling real-time data logging, remote access, and digital communication.

Unlike traditional recorders, modern sensors eliminate subjective interpretation and offer standardized, high-accuracy measurements.

Many meet the performance criteria outlined in ISO 9060:2018 and the WMO Guide to Meteorological Instruments and Methods of Observation (WMO-No. 8) (World Meteorological Organization, 2008).

A modern resource station is really one tracker with three jobs: it aims the pyrheliometer at the sun (DNI), positions the shading ball over the diffuse pyranometer (DHI), and the fixed pyranometer reads GHI. The redundancy is deliberate — the closure equation GHI ≈ DNI·cos(θz) + DHI is computed continuously, and any drift among the three instruments flags a calibration or soiling problem automatically.

Numbers to memorize as a set: 5.7° (pyrheliometer field of view), 180° (pyranometer), 120 W/m² (WMO bright-sunshine threshold), 1-minute (modern resolution), ISO 9060:2018 Class A (instrument class), WMO-No. 8 (the methods guide). Exam questions love these constants.

Thinking the shadow band/ring/ball blocks diffuse light. It blocks the direct beam, so that only the diffuse sky component reaches the sensor. Another trap: quoting the sunshine threshold as 1,200 or 12 W/m² — it is 120 W/m² per WMO guidelines.

Be ready to name the three shading devices (shadow band, shading ring, shading ball), the two electronic sunshine sensor types (silicon photodiode, thermopile), and the three uses of diffuse data (PV design under cloudy conditions, validating radiative transfer models, clearness index / resource quality).

When a Bangladeshi solar IPP signs a power purchase agreement, its lenders typically require an on-site ISO 9060:2018 Class A station logging GHI, DHI, DNI and sunshine duration at 1-minute intervals for at least 12 months — exactly the instrument suite of this lecture, deployed as a condition of financing.

The 120 W/m² threshold was chosen by the WMO so that electronic sensors would reproduce, on average, the burn behaviour of the Campbell-Stokes card — the new standard was deliberately calibrated to preserve continuity with 140 years of burnt-card records.

Satellite retrieval is an inverse problem: the satellite never measures surface irradiance — it measures radiance leaving the top of the atmosphere in chosen bands, then a model inverts that signal (using cloud brightness in the visible, cloud-top temperature in the IR, and surface albedo) into surface insolation. Every satellite product therefore inherits model assumptions, which is why validation against pyranometer stations remains mandatory.

Band logic made simple: visible channel = how bright the cloud tops are (more reflection up ⇒ less sunshine down); IR channel = how cold the cloud tops are (colder ⇒ taller/thicker cloud). Two independent clues, one inversion to surface insolation.

Assuming satellite data (e.g., NASA POWER) can replace ground measurement for bankability. A 0.5° × 0.5° cell spans ~55 km — it smooths local microclimate, and its values are model estimates. Lenders require on-site Class A instruments; satellites serve prospecting, gap-filling and long-term context.

Memorize the three research threads exactly as cited via (Stine and Geyer, 2001): Diak et al. (1982) — half-hourly satellite images, visible + IR bands; Cano et al. (1986) — general method for global solar radiation from meteorological data; Ineichen & Perez (1999, printed "Prerz" in the slide) — prediction from ground reflectance (albedo) data. Plus NASA POWER: 2003, outgrowth of the Surface meteorology and Solar Energy (SSE) project, 0.5° grid, inversion to surface insolation.

A common Bangladeshi workflow: pull 20+ years of NASA POWER insolation for the candidate upazila (free, instant), shortlist sites, then install a ground station for 12 months and use it to bias-correct the long satellite record — the "measure-correlate-predict" method borrowed from wind engineering.

The same geostationary weather satellites whose images appear in TV forecasts are the source of most solar resource maps: one platform, imaging every half hour, quietly serves both meteorology and the global solar industry.

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • Obtaining the solar radiation data is very important — instruments exist for global, beam, diffuse and sunshine hours, and satellite remote sensing can also be applied.
  • A pyranometer — "a type of actinometer used to measure broadband solar irradiance on a planar surface" (Duffie and Beckman, 2013) — reads global irradiance (W/m²) over a hemispherical 180° field of view, including both direct and diffuse.
  • A pyrheliometer measures only the direct beam through a narrow collimated field of view (~5.7°), via a precision aperture and thermopile, and must ride a solar tracker.
  • The Campbell-Stokes recorder (Campbell 1853, Stokes 1879) burns a trace on a calibrated card; burn length ↔ bright-sunshine duration over 24 h.
  • Modern practice: shaded pyranometers (shadow band/ring/ball) for diffuse; dual-axis-tracked Class A pyrheliometers for DNI; electronic sunshine sensors with the WMO 120 W/m² threshold; ISO 9060:2018 and WMO-No. 8 govern quality.
  • Satellite remote sensing (visible + IR imaging, inversion to surface insolation) and NASA POWER (2003, 0.5° × 0.5° grid) complement ground stations.

🛠 Engineering Applications

  • PV design & performance evaluation → pyranometer (GHI, POA).
  • CSP & solar thermal design, bankability → tracked pyrheliometer (DNI).
  • Cloudy-climate PV, radiative-transfer validation, clearness index → shaded pyranometer (DHI).
  • Long climate records & Angström correlations → sunshine recorders.
  • Prospecting & gap-filling anywhere → satellite products (NASA POWER).
