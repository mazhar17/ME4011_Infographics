R1
GHI, DNI, DHI and Their Closure (Parts 3–5)
GHI = DNI cos θ + DHI — the columns every dataset serves
▸

The three irradiance quantities you will meet in every dataset table: GHI on the horizontal (direct + diffuse together), DNI normal to the sun's rays (the beam), and DHI scattered from the whole sky dome. They close as:

GHI = DNI cos θ + DHI
  • Memory aid: "G for the Ground total, N for a tube aimed at the suN, D for the Dome." Today's portals serve these three as their core columns.
  • Examination tip: the θ in the closure is the solar zenith angle — the projection factor that puts DNI onto the horizontal.
  • Common mistake: summing DNI + DHI directly and calling it GHI — the cos θ projection is not optional.
  • Conceptual relationship: Part 4 measured these; Part 5 transposed them; today you download them.
R2
Units in Dataset Tables
W/m² (rate) vs. kWh/m²/day (energy) vs. kWh/kWp (specific yield)
▸
W/m²
Instantaneous irradiance — the unit of hourly TMY records (GHI, DNI, DHI columns).
kWh/m²/day
Daily-integrated radiation — the unit of monthly-average dataset tables (POWER, PVGIS outputs).
kWh/kWp
Specific yield — energy produced per installed kilowatt-peak; the Global Solar Atlas serves it as PVOUT.
  • Memory aid: "per m² = resource; per kWp = product." PVOUT already includes typical system behaviour.
  • Practical shortcut: annual PVOUT ≈ 1,400–1,500 kWh/kWp is a typical Bangladeshi figure — good for sanity-checking downloads.
  • Common mistake: comparing a kWh/m² resource number against a kWh/kWp yield number as if they were the same quantity.
  • Quick reminder: hourly file ⇒ W/m²; monthly table ⇒ kWh/m²/day; investor map ⇒ kWh/kWp.
R3
TMY from Part 3 — About to Go Deep
Typical year · simulation input · not for extremes
▸

Part 3 introduced the Typical Meteorological Year: one year of hourly data assembled from a multiyear record to represent typical conditions, used as the standard weather input for computer simulation of solar energy conversion and building systems — and never for worst-case design. Today's TMY section descends into the file itself: formats, header fields, and how to actually download one.

  • Memory aid: TMY = "Twelve Median-months, Yearly" — 12 most-typical months stitched into one synthetic year.
  • Examination tip: the two ever-green TMY questions are its primary application (simulation) and its design limitation (not extremes).
  • Conceptual relationship: the NSRDB Viewer introduced today is the machine that generates and serves these files.
R4
Tilt & Monsoon (Part 5 + Parts 3–4)
Why portals ask for an angle · what monsoon does to the beam
▸

Part 5 taught the transposition to tilted planes — which is why PVGIS asks for a slope and reports "Global irradiation at angle:". And from Parts 3–4: under Dhaka's monsoon the direct beam collapses while diffuse light persists — the seasonal behaviour today's Hands-On quantifies with the diffuse fraction DHI/GHI.

  • Quick reminder: in-plane/tilted values from a portal already contain Part 5's three-component transposition.
  • Engineering intuition: a high diffuse fraction means "the sky glows everywhere" — pointing hardware (trackers) has little to point at.
  • Frequently confused: diffuse fraction is DHI/GHI (a ratio, 0–1), not DHI itself (W/m²).

Understanding GHI, DNI, and DHI in Solar Energy

Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI)
Total solar radiation received on a horizontal surface · Comprises both direct and diffuse components · Mathematically expressed as:
GHI = DNI cos θ + DHI   (1)
Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI)
Solar radiation received directly from the sun · Measured on a surface normal to the sun's rays · Strongly reduced by clouds, dust, and haze
Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance (DHI)
Solar radiation scattered by the atmosphere · Reaches the surface from all directions · Dominant during cloudy and monsoon conditions

Resource uncertainty propagates straight into money: a ±5% GHI uncertainty becomes roughly ±5% energy, which at typical debt ratios can swing a project's equity return by several percentage points. That is why lenders price data quality — a "bankable" dataset literally lowers the cost of capital.

Tie the three dataset classes to the three project stages as a single ladder: satellite-based ↔ conceptual design, model-assimilated ↔ detailed engineering, bankable-grade ↔ bankability assessment. Most exam answers about "which dataset?" are climbs up or down this ladder.

Treating "free" as "low quality" and "commercial" as "always necessary." The lecture's six portals are all freely accessible, and one of them (Global Solar Atlas) serves bankable-grade data — the ladder is about validation and uncertainty, not price alone.

Memorize the three consequences of poor data selection exactly as listed: oversized systems, financial losses, performance shortfall — and the trade-off triple: accuracy, resolution, accessibility and cost.

A Bangladeshi IPP typically walks the full ladder on one project: POWER for the first spreadsheet, Global Solar Atlas maps for the investor deck, NSRDB/PVGIS TMY files for PVsyst simulation, and finally a ground campaign to close bankability.

MERRA-2 — the reanalysis behind NASA POWER — re-simulates the entire global atmosphere from 1980 onward, assimilating millions of observations per day. When you download a POWER time series, you are reading the output of one of the largest continuous physics simulations ever run.

🗂 TMY at a Glance (after the lecture's infographic)

Understanding TMY Data — a composite year of median weather

  • Definition: a one-year, hourly dataset representing typical, not extreme, conditions.
  • Constructed from multi-year analysis: twelve individual months are selected from a long-term period that best represent median conditions.
  • Primary application — system simulation: the standard input for computer simulations of solar energy conversion and building systems.
  • Unsuitable for extreme-case design: not for designing systems to withstand worst-case weather scenarios.

Composition of TMY Datasets — key data parameters

  • Solar irradiance: Global Horizontal (GHI), Direct Normal (DNI), Diffuse Horizontal (DHI) — all in W/m².
  • Location: latitude and longitude in decimal degrees; elevation in meters.
  • Meteorological: temperature (°C), wind speed (m/s), atmospheric pressure (millibar).

The TMY is a brilliant compression: decades of weather squeezed into 8,760 rows that preserve the statistics that matter for energy (medians, daily patterns, seasonality) while discarding what doesn't (specific storms, record heat waves). Simulation software exploits exactly this: one fast year-run approximates the long-term average yield.

For the header lists, memorize the shape rather than reciting: line 1 = "who/where" (source, IDs, place names, coordinates, zones, elevation); line 2 = "when + weather" (5 time fields, then dew point, the three irradiances DHI–DNI–GHI, then temperature, pressure, wind direction/speed, surface albedo). Note the irradiances arrive in alphabetical order: DHI, DNI, GHI.

Using a TMY to size structures or drainage — "TMYs represent typical rather than extreme conditions." A second trap: assuming all TMY files look alike; TMY1, TMY2, TMY3 and SAM CSV differ in layout, so simulation software must be told which format it is reading.

Three near-certain items: the definition quote ("one year of hourly data that best represents median weather conditions over a multiyear period"), the use-and-limitation pair, and naming the four formats (TMY1, TMY2, TMY3, SAM CSV) plus the three NSRDB access routes (direct download, API, Globus).

Download a TMY for Dhaka from the NSRDB Viewer as SAM CSV, drop it into SAM or PVsyst, and the software immediately produces monthly and annual yield estimates — the exact workflow this lecture equips you to perform, and the standard first deliverable of any PV design internship.

The .epw format the NSRDB can emit stands for "EnergyPlus Weather" — the same TMY files that size solar farms also drive building energy simulations of offices and hospitals, one file serving two industries.

Diffuse Radiation Dominance During Monsoon in Dhaka

Interactive recreation of the lecture figure — "Diffuse fraction (DHI/GHI) for Dhaka based on typical monsoon climate behavior". Hover the bars; toggle the tracker overlay.

  • Diffuse radiation fraction varies strongly with season
  • Jan–Feb: Clearer skies — Diffuse fraction ≈ 0.30–0.40
  • Mar–May: Pre-monsoon transition — Increasing cloud cover · Diffuse fraction ≈ 0.40–0.50
  • Jun–Sep: Monsoon period — Persistent cloud cover · Diffuse fraction ≈ 0.60–0.70
  • Oct–Nov: Retreating monsoon — Gradual recovery of direct radiation
Key Insight: In monsoon-dominated climates like Bangladesh, a large fraction of GHI consists of diffuse radiation, reducing the contribution of DNI.
Engineering Implication: High diffuse radiation during monsoon months significantly reduces the effectiveness of solar tracking systems and limits performance gains from tilt optimization.

✅ Replicate-It-Online Checklist

    The Hands-On teaches a professional habit: always convert a portal's monthly table into the diffuse fraction before making technology decisions. Two sites with identical annual GHI can hide opposite beam/diffuse structures — and opposite answers on trackers, concentrators and tilt sensitivity.

    Memorize Dhaka's diffuse-fraction staircase as four steps: 0.3–0.4 (Jan–Feb) → 0.4–0.5 (Mar–May) → 0.6–0.7 (Jun–Sep) → recovery (Oct–Nov). The monsoon step is the exam favourite.

    Averaging the year and concluding "trackers gain ~25% in Bangladesh." Tracking gain collapses precisely in the months (Jun–Sep) when the diffuse fraction hits 0.60–0.70 — the annual average hides the seasonal failure the lecture's Engineering Implication warns about.
